# EXP-008: Digital Gold Market Research

> **One-liner**: Discovery research — we're testing whether Pakistani merchants have real demand to sell digital gold, and whether the jeweler markup savings argument is the unlock.

> **Classification**: This is **discovery research**, not an intervention experiment. There is no controlled variable — we're running structured interviews to surface whether latent demand exists. Results tell us if the behavior *can* exist, not that it *will* persist. Do not treat findings as validation of a scalable product.

## Signal Framework

Single metrics are gameable. Pakistani shopkeeper hospitality means "haan bhai" is the default response, not a signal. This framework tiers signals by how hard they are to fake.

| Tier | Signal | Why it's credible | Minimum bar |
|------|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Behavioral** (hardest to fake) | Unprompted actions — asks for launch date without being prompted, offers phone number before asked, calls over another shopkeeper, asks operational specifics (commission mechanics, settlement timing, minimum amounts) | Requires genuine cognitive engagement — can't be produced by politeness alone | ≥3 merchants across all interviews show unprompted demand signals |
| **Economic argument** | Sentiment shift at Q4 — merchant reaction changes after hearing jeweler markup savings (10-15%, Rs. 50-80K/tola) | Tests a specific, falsifiable value prop. Reaction is observed in real-time, not recalled. | ≥50% of interviewees react positively at Q4 |
| **Distribution willingness** | "Would you sell this?" = Yes (Q6) | Tests the merchant-as-channel model specifically, not just gold interest in general | ≥40% willing to earn commission by selling |
| **Confirmation** (48-hour callback) | Pickup → remembers pitch → accepts WhatsApp group for early access | Filters performative interest. If they forgot the pitch in 48 hours, the interest was fake. | ≥30% of phone-number-givers pass all 3 gates |

**Noise floor** (do NOT count as interest): Asking 1-2 generic questions, saying "achha hai," nodding along, signing up for waitlist after being asked directly. This is the Pakistani hospitality baseline.

### How to read the matrix

- **3+ signals green**: Strong discovery signal. Recommend leadership proceeds to product scoping.
- **2 signals green**: Moderate signal. Recommend Round 3 with refined positioning before product commitment.
- **1 or 0 signals green**: Weak/no signal. Recommend shelving digital gold for merchant distribution.

### Bull Case (what gold advocates valued)

1. **Investment vehicle** — gold as wealth-building asset, price appreciation without jeweler premiums
2. **Fractional access** — "even Rs. 500" democratizes gold ownership. One shopkeeper independently identified this as "the best idea"
3. **Price transparency** — direct market rate access without jeweler intermediation

### Bear Case (5 ranked barriers)

| Rank | Barrier | Mentions | Implication |
|------|---------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | "I need gold in my hands" — physical possession anxiety | 4 | Redemption mechanics must be crystal clear |
| 2 | Legitimacy & trust concerns — previous exposure to fraudulent apps | 3 | Need social proof, backing info, vault verification |
| 3 | Vague redemption process — Round 1 only said gold "can come in physical form" | 3 | Must explain: vault in Dubai, delivery to door in ~1 week |
| 4 | Security & counterparty risk — vault reliability, insurance | 2 | Address geopolitical exposure, insurance coverage |
| 5 | Unfamiliar product category — dollars map to existing mobile money, gold doesn't | 2 | Need new mental models, can't assume transfer from dollar understanding |

### Critical Gap: The Untested Value Prop

The strongest economic argument — **eliminating the 10-15% jeweler markup (Rs. 50,000-80,000 per tola savings)** — was completely absent from Round 1 interviews. No shopkeeper was told about this. Round 1 tested a vague concept; Round 2 tests a specific product pitch with this argument as the centerpiece.

### Demographic Patterns

- Gold advocates skewed slightly older (maturity/wealth-preservation positioning)
- PWD zones showed highest gold receptivity AND deepest polarization — potential pilot territory
- Gold advocates evaluated it as an **investment vehicle**; dollar advocates viewed it as **payment utility**. Fundamentally different use-case evaluations, not comparable products.

## Round 2 Design

### Core Question

Round 1 tested a vague concept. Round 2 tests whether a **specific, detailed pitch** — built around the jeweler markup savings — moves merchant sentiment from skeptical to interested.

### Setup

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Sample | 40 interviews across two parallel arms |
| ISB/RWP arm | 20 EP/JC agent shops — Islamabad & Rawalpindi; Feb 21–22, 2026; Surveyor A; audio + transcripts |
| Karachi arm | 20 communications shops — Clifton, DHA, Gulshan-e-Iqbal, Malir; Feb 24–25, 2026; Faisal Soomro; form data only (no audio) |
| Duration per interview | 5-7 minutes |
| Budget | Rs. 20,000 per arm |
| Isolates gold | No dollars comparison — gold-only pitch |

### Round 2 City Mapping

| Karachi Area | # Shops | ISB/RWP Analog |
|-------------|---------|----------------|
| Clifton | 5 | F-8/F-6 ISB — upscale mixed |
| DHA Phase 1–6 | 3 | No direct ISB equivalent — upscale residential strips |
| Gulshan-e-Iqbal | 12 | I-10/G-10/G-13 ISB — dense middle-class commercial markaz |

**Cross-city pair for apples-to-apples comparison:** Gulshan-e-Iqbal (12/20) vs ISB I-10/G-10 cluster.

### Sequential Interview Format

The interview is structured as a **sentiment funnel** — three pitch sections with sentiment captured after each. This lets us measure *which argument moves the needle*, not just whether the final answer is "yes."

#### Part 1: Baseline (1 min)

| Q | Question | What it measures |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| Q1 | "Do your customers ever talk about buying gold?" | Existing demand signal — is gold already in the shopkeeper's world? |
| Q2 | "Do you know how much extra a jeweler charges over market price?" | Awareness baseline — do they know about the markup before we tell them? |

#### Part 2: The Three-Section Pitch (2 min)

**Section A — Core concept:**
> "Real gold on your phone. Stored in a vault in Dubai. Buy for as little as Rs. 500."

| Q | Question | What it measures |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| Q3 | "What's your first reaction?" | Baseline sentiment before economics. Captures gut response to "digital gold" as a concept. |

**Section B — Premium argument (THE CRITICAL TEST):**
> "Jewelers charge 10-15% over market price. That's Rs. 50,000-80,000 extra per tola. This app gives you the real price."

| Q | Question | What it measures |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| Q4 | "If customers could skip the jeweler markup, would they be interested?" | **This is the centerpiece metric.** Does the economic argument shift sentiment? Captures the delta between Q3 (vague concept) and Q4 (specific value prop). |

**Section C — Redemption clarity:**
> "Want it in hand? Request delivery. It ships from the vault to your address in about a week."

| Q | Question | What it measures |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| Q5 | "Does knowing how you get it back change how you feel?" | Tests whether the #1 Round 1 barrier ("I need gold in my hands") is addressable with clear logistics. |

#### Part 3: Shopkeeper Distribution (2 min)

| Q | Question | What it measures |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| Q6 | "If you could earn commission selling this, would you?" | Distribution channel viability — would merchants actually sell it? |
| Q7 | "What would you need to see before you're comfortable selling?" (open-ended) | Barrier identification — what's between "interested" and "acting"? |
| Q8 | "Could you convince a customer with: 'Buy gold for Rs. 1,000 — real gold, better price than the jeweler, delivered when you want'?" | Pitch relay test — can the merchant articulate the value prop themselves? |

### Commission Handling

Round 2 leads with the **customer value prop** (markup savings), not merchant commission. When commission comes up:
- Open-ended in Q6: "If you could earn commission" — tests willingness without requiring a specific number
- If merchant presses: "Agar customer 10,000 ka sona khareede, aapko 100-300 rupay milenge" (concrete range)
- **Open assumption for product**: Exact commission rate must be resolved before any Round 3 or product scoping

### Per-Interview Deliverables

- [ ] Audio recording
- [ ] Google Maps pin (shop location)
- [ ] Structured data form (all Q1-Q8 responses)
- [ ] Shop photograph

## Confirmation Metric: 48-Hour Callback

Every merchant who gives their phone number gets called 48 hours after the interview. This filters performative interest from real demand.

### Callback Flow

| Gate | Question/Action | What it filters |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Gate 1: Pickup** | Call 2x at different times | Availability (not a signal of interest — but 0 pickups = 0 real data) |
| **Gate 2: Recall** | "We spoke about digital gold 2 days ago — do you remember?" | Performative interest. If they forgot, the conversation didn't register. |
| **Gate 3: Micro-commitment** | "We're creating a WhatsApp group for merchants who want early access. Can I add you?" | Real interest requires action. Joining a WhatsApp group is low-cost but requires saying yes to a specific thing, not a vague "I'm interested." |

### Interpretation

| Callback result | What it means |
|----------------|---------------|
| >30% pass all 3 gates | Interest is real. Merchants remembered AND committed. |
| >30% pick up but <15% recall | Interest was performative. The pitch didn't stick. |
| <15% pick up | Inconclusive — could be phone culture, timing, or disinterest. Don't over-interpret. |

## Decision Rules

Present findings report with recommendation to leadership. Decision authority: Turab / Brandon.

| Signal Matrix Result | Recommendation | What it means |
|---------------------|----------------|---------------|
| 3-4 signals green | **PROCEED** — recommend product scoping sprint for digital gold feature | Strong discovery signal across behavioral, economic, distribution, and confirmation dimensions |
| 2 signals green | **ITERATE** — recommend Round 3 with modified positioning before product commitment | Partial signal. Identify which argument worked and which didn't. Design Round 3 to test the gap. |
| 0-1 signals green | **SHELVE** — recommend deprioritizing digital gold for merchant distribution | Insufficient demand signal. Document learnings. Revisit only if market conditions change (gold price surge, competitor entry). |

### Confirmation metric override

If the primary signals look strong BUT the 48-hour callback fails (<15% pass all 3 gates), downgrade the recommendation by one tier. Strong in-person reactions that evaporate in 48 hours are not real demand.

## Assumption Check

**1. What has to be true for this research to produce useful signal?**
- Merchants must understand "digital gold" well enough to react authentically (not just nod at a concept they can't picture)
- The interviewer must deliver the pitch sections consistently — each section builds on the prior one
- Islamabad EP/JC shops must be comparable enough to Rawalpindi shops that cross-city patterns are meaningful

**2. What's the riskiest assumption?**
That expressed interest during a 5-7 minute interview translates to willingness to act when a product exists. Even with the callback confirmation, there's a gap between "I'd join a WhatsApp group" and "I'll actively sell digital gold to customers." This is inherent to discovery research — we're testing whether demand *can* exist, not whether it *will* at scale.

**3. How can we test that cheaply?**
The callback IS the cheap test. Rs. 20,000 for 20 interviews + phone calls. If the callback conversion is strong, the next cheap test is a mock transaction demo (show a prototype, have the merchant "sell" gold to the interviewer) before committing product resources.

**4. Does this create perverse incentives?**
- Rs. 1,000 consent fee per interview creates slight incentive to participate but not to express specific interest (fee is for participation, not positive response)
- Risk: merchant tells you what you want to hear to be polite → mitigated by tiered signal framework and callback confirmation
- Risk: interviewer unconsciously leads toward positive responses → mitigated by structured questions and audio recording for audit

**5. Does it require ambassador skill/judgment?**
Yes — the interviewer must deliver three pitch sections consistently and capture reactions accurately. Variance between interviewers is a real risk. Mitigation: use the same 1-2 interviewers for all 20 Round 2 interviews. Audit via audio recordings.

## Discovery Validity

This is not a statistical validity check — it's an honesty check about what n=34 qualitative interviews can and can't tell you.

### What this CAN tell us

- Whether any merchant shows genuine, unprompted demand for digital gold (existence proof)
- Which specific argument (fractional access, markup savings, redemption clarity) shifts sentiment most
- What the top barriers are and whether they're addressable
- Whether the merchant-as-distribution-channel model has any traction
- What positioning/framing language resonates

### What this CANNOT tell us

- Demand magnitude (what % of all Pakistani merchants would sell gold — n=34 can't answer this)
- Conversion rates (what % of interested merchants would actually sell — need a real product for this)
- Price sensitivity (what commission rate makes merchants act — need real transactions)
- Customer demand (we're testing merchant willingness to sell, not end-user willingness to buy)

### Why n=54 is appropriate

Discovery research follows information saturation, not statistical power. In qualitative research, new themes typically stop emerging after 12-15 interviews in a homogeneous population (Pakistani shopkeepers in urban markets). At n=54 (14 Round 1 + 20 ISB/RWP + 20 Karachi), we're well past saturation for theme identification. We're NOT trying to estimate a population proportion — we're trying to identify patterns, barriers, and positioning. The Karachi arm adds value by testing geographic generalizability of ISB/RWP findings.

### Key Karachi-Specific Findings

**What's stronger in Karachi:** Q4 interest rate 2× ISB (60% vs 40%) — markup argument lands harder. Distribution willingness also higher (70% vs 40%).

**What's the same:** Authenticity certificate demanded by ~15/20 in both cities. Physical possession preference. Fraud/scam frame dominant.

**Unique Karachi barriers (not prominent in ISB/RWP):**
1. **Shop-type mismatch** — 12/20 explicitly stated "meri mobile ki dukaan hai, iska taluq sonay se hai hi nai"
2. **Purchasing-power ceiling** (K-07, K-11) — customer base argues over Rs 10-15 price differences
3. **Robbery paranoia** (K-11) — fears word getting out that shop holds digital gold

### Recommendation

**ITERATE** — Combined n=40 delivers 2 green signals (distribution willingness 55%, behavioral demand). Q4 shift is at threshold (50%). The shop-type mismatch finding from Karachi suggests that Round 3 should target gold-adjacent shops (jewelers, pawn shops, savings agents) rather than generic communications shops. The authenticity/fraud barrier remains constant across both cities and requires institutional resolution.

### Key Learnings

1. The jeweler markup argument works better in Karachi than ISB (60% vs 40% Q4 shift) — possibly because Karachi merchants have more daily exposure to gold transactions and price fluctuations.
2. Shop-type mismatch is the dominant Karachi barrier — 12/20 explicitly said their shop has nothing to do with gold. This barrier barely appeared in ISB/RWP. Round 3 must qualify shop type more carefully or pivot to gold-adjacent shops.
3. Authenticity certificate demand is universal and consistent across both cities — this is not resolvable through pitch improvements alone; it requires a physical artifact or verifiable institutional anchor.

### Feed-Forward

> Round 3 hypothesis: A trust-resolved pitch (institutional anchor + vault certificate physical artifact) delivered to gold-adjacent shops (jewelers, savings agents, pawn shops) in both cities would achieve ≥50% Q4 shift and ≥30% callback conversion. Proposed: EXP-008 Round 3.

## Context

### What prompted this research

Gold is rallying globally (all-time highs in 2024-2025). Gold is the default savings/investment vehicle in South Asia — culturally embedded in weddings, inflation hedging, and emergency funds. No competitor in Pakistan offers digital gold through merchant networks. ZAR's existing merchant distribution infrastructure could be a moat if gold demand is real.

The core business question: can ZAR's merchant network — built for dollar exchange — also distribute digital gold? If yes, it's a second revenue line with zero incremental distribution cost. If no, don't invest product resources.

| Prior Work | What we learned | How it informs EXP-008 |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
