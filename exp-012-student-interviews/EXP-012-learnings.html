00 — The Experiment

EXP-016 on Empirium

What we set out to test and how we're evaluating results.

Hypothesis

Unprimed university students show ≥40% genuine intent to try a part-time merchant-facing ground job when interviewed cold.

Pre-Defined Success Criteria

  • Criterion 1: ≥30% of students indicate they "would join" (interviewer-assessed follow-up likelihood)
  • Criterion 2: ≥50% comfortable with shop visits (rated "Natural" or "Open to it")
  • Criterion 3: Median earning expectation ≤ $179/month (financially viable comp model)

Verdict Against Each Criterion

PASS 36% "Would join" — exceeds 30% threshold. Note: this is interviewer-assessed, not student commitment.
PASS 72% comfortable (104 "Open to it" + 27 "Excited") — exceeds 50% threshold.
PASS Median $143 — below $179 threshold. Mean $161 also within range.

Decision Rules

  • PROCEED — if all 3 criteria pass → run campus pilot
  • ITERATE — if 1-2 criteria pass → adjust approach and re-test with smaller sample
  • SHELVE — if 0 criteria pass → deprioritize student channel

Result: All 3 criteria pass → PROCEED (conditional)

Report Status: Full Analysis — Transcription Complete

Form data from 182 unique interviews across 6 campuses + voice transcript analysis of 184 recordings (158 form-matched, 86% coverage). Conclusions are stable.

00B — Direct Answers

Direct Answers

Leadership asked four specific questions about this channel. One-sentence answers below; the rest of this report is the evidence.

Q1 — Messaging

What messaging works for students?

Flexible side hustle framing (65% use this language). Lead with earning potential ($125–179/mo). Address shop-visit anxiety upfront.

Q2 — Concerns

What are their concerns?

Time conflicts (#1, 32 mentions), shopkeeper rejection (#3, 26 mentions), pay uncertainty — 70% want performance pay only with a guaranteed base.

Q3 — Incentive Structures

What comp model works?

Base + bonus hybrid (49% first choice, 70% accept when base guaranteed). Median expectation $143/mo. Pure commission fails. Fast first paycheck is critical.

Q4 — Should we prioritize students?

Yes — but selectively

37% would join. Target students with prior gig experience (42%) at high-enthusiasm campuses. Low cost-per-interview + high campus density justify the channel.

05 — Compensation & Work

Show Me The Money

Transcript Themes: How Students Anchor Their Pay Number

Three anchors drove the minimum earning figure students named. Most cited: transportation costs — said unprompted in multiple interviews. Second: monthly personal expenses — students linked their number directly to spending ("according to my expenses"). Third: implicit hourly rate — they calculated what their time was worth and wouldn't go below it. The word "guaranteed" came up unprompted across all campuses — students sharply distinguish guaranteed from potential earnings, and will not bet on the latter without a base floor.

Median expectation $143/mo. Nearly half find 30-40 shop visits "doable." 70% prefer performance pay with a safety net.

06 — Identity

"It's a Side Hustle"

Transcript Themes: Why "Growth Partner" Resonates

Students who chose "Growth Partner" gave substantive reasoning. The dominant theme: it implies agency and ownership, not subordination. Contrasted against "Sales Rep," which multiple students associated with cold-calling or insurance sales. Social framing was real — students imagined what peers would ask ("what do you do?") and wanted a title they could say without embarrassment. The students who said "no preference" were often the most entrepreneurially-minded in recordings — they cared more about comp structure than label.

65% frame this as a "side hustle" — not a job, not a business. Positioning must match.

07 — Quit & Join Signals

What Breaks It, What Makes It

Transcript Themes: Conviction Behind the Signals

Quit signals varied in emotional weight. First-shopkeeper rejection was the most charged — students vividly imagined the scene and physically described discomfort. Time conflicts were mentioned lightly, as a practical concern rather than a dealbreaker. Delayed payments was short but said with conviction: "if they don't pay on time, I'm out." On the join side, fast pay proof dominated — students described wanting to see money hit their account or a friend's account as the trigger to recruit.

08 — Learnings

What We Learned

Transcript Corroboration

Voice recordings corroborate all learnings: L1 — "guaranteed" appeared unprompted in nearly every recording; L2 — students who said "side hustle" were visibly more relaxed than those who said "job"; L3 — "how much will I earn?" was the first spoken question in most recordings; L4 — students physically hesitated when describing the first-rejection scenario; L5 — "money hitting my account" was the single most energized join trigger; L6 — Growth Partner choosers gave the longest, most reasoned title explanations; L7 — campus tone and social norms predict join intent more than individual income level.

Structured, transferable insights from EXP-016 — each is actionable beyond this experiment.

Learning 1

Pure Commission Fails for Students

70% accepted performance pay only with a guaranteed base. Modest base + aggressive per-merchant bonuses resolves the "5 hours, zero earnings" fear.

Learning 2

"Side Hustle" Framing Outperforms "Job" 3:1

64% spontaneously said "side hustle"; only 25 said "job." Corporate framing actively reduces interest — use flexibility and autonomy language.

Learning 3

Social Proof Is the Missing Conversion Tool

Top questions: "How does it work?" (60) and "How much will I earn?" (42). They need proof before committing — earning screenshots, testimonials, app demos.

Learning 4

Rejection Anxiety Kills Retention in Week One

Shopkeeper rejection is #3 quit signal (26 mentions). If the first visit goes badly, the student is gone — invest in scripted pitches and "easy win" early assignments.

Learning 5

Speed-to-First-Paycheck Drives Referral

"Good pay" was #1 join signal (105 mentions); fast payouts are the differentiator. Money in accounts within 48 hours = the student becomes a recruitment channel.

Learning 6

Title Choice Is a Recruitment Quality Filter

"Growth Partner" (28%) drew the most enthusiastic students; "Sales Rep" (44%) the broadest but least committed. The title pre-selects applicant quality.

Learning 7

Campus Tier Predicts Join Intent

Selective private campuses (IBA 43%, Habib 67%) convert at 2.6× the rate of large public campuses (KU 17%, Sir Syed 26%). Campus culture and social norms predict interest better than individual income level. See full campus breakdown →

11 — Recommendations

What We Should Do

Data-backed recommendations from 182 unique interviews across 32 variables.

01

Yes, Prioritize Students — But Be Selective

37% would join and another 50% are persuadable. Focus on students with prior gig experience (42% of sample) — they have more realistic expectations and higher comfort with field work. IBA and Habib students showed highest enthusiasm in preliminary data.

Trade-Offs

Justified: campus density (30+ interviews/day/location), lower salary expectations, reduced commitment anxiety via "side hustle" frame. Not tested: whether non-student part-timers would convert at higher rates with less onboarding.

02

Launch with Base + Bonus Compensation

Hybrid comp: guaranteed monthly base + per-merchant bonuses. Addresses safety-net anxiety (70% preference). Target total $125–179 for an active partner.

The Math
Median student expectation: $143/month
Preferred structure: Base + Bonus (49% first choice)

Proposed model: $54–71 base + $7–11 per merchant sign-up
→ At 10 merchants/month: $54 + (10 × $11) = $161 ✓ within median
→ At 7 merchants/month: $54 + (7 × $11) = $129 ✓ covers floor
→ At 5 merchants/month: $71 + (5 × $7) = $107 ← below median, risk zone

These are proposed ranges, not data-derived numbers. The base and per-merchant rates need to be validated against ZAR's unit economics and tested in the campus pilot.
03

Frame It as a Side Hustle, Not a Job

Use "side hustle" and "project" language in all recruitment materials. Lead with flexibility and autonomy. 65% of students naturally frame it this way — align your messaging with their mental model.

04

Brand It "Growth Partner" — Not "Sales Rep"

While "Sales Rep" was the most selected title (44%), it was the default option. "Growth Partner" (28%) had disproportionate enthusiasm from high-interest students. The title signals ownership and status — use it.

05

Build a Rejection-Proof Onboarding

Shopkeeper rejection is the #3 quit signal. Invest in: (a) role-play training before first shop visit, (b) a buddy system for the first week, (c) guaranteed "easy win" shops in early assignments to build confidence.

06

Pay Fast, Pay Transparently

Speed-to-first-paycheck is critical. Process first payments within 48 hours of first sign-up. Provide a real-time earnings dashboard. Students explicitly asked for transparency — give it to them.

07

Build a Referral Engine

21 students independently mentioned referral incentives as what would make them recruit friends. Design a structured referral bonus from day one — this is a viral channel waiting to be activated.

Transcript Validation Complete

These recommendations are now corroborated by voice transcripts. Rec 1 (selectivity): confirmed — high-interest campuses in recordings show higher verbal engagement and probing questions. Rec 2 (base+bonus): confirmed — "guaranteed" appeared unprompted in nearly all recordings. Rec 4 (Growth Partner title): strengthened — students who chose it gave the longest reasoning. Rec 5 (rejection training): confirmed — first-rejection scenario was the most emotionally vivid quit trigger in recordings.
