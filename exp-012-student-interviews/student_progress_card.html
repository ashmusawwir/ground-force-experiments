What We've Learnt So Far
  • The interest pool is real but conditionally committed. 90% showed Medium+ interest — and of the remaining, 62% of interviewees still had one reservation: their conversion depends entirely on first-week design: clear pay, manageable targets, no ugly surprises.
  • "Pure commission" triggers fear; a base unlocks performance appetite. 62% accept lower base + more per sign-up — but only when a guaranteed floor exists. The winning structure is a meaningful base (PKR 15–20K) + per-merchant bonuses targeting PKR 35–45K total.
  • Shopkeeper rejection — not pay — is the #1 quit trigger. 27% cite hostile/unresponsive shopkeepers as the thing that makes them walk. Training must lead with rejection reframing, not product knowledge.
  • The app is their personal credibility proxy. Students lend their reputation to the product. Several said they'd quit if the app crashes mid-demo — because it makes them look bad. A broken onboarding flow costs you the student, not just the sign-up.
  • Referral runs on proof of payment, not promises. 47% of referral motivators reduce to "show me the money first." The most effective next-cohort recruitment is paying the first wave well and on time — then letting them recruit their friends.
  • Female DPT students face structural barriers to field sales. 5 of 30 KU interviewees explicitly cited societal or safety norms as barriers to entering shops alone. This sub-group needs a pairing model or different role design — solo field outreach is a non-starter for them.
What We're Going to Improve
  • 1
    Increase cold outreach participation
    Currently 82% friend/referral, near-zero cold contacts. Target: ≥50% cold in remaining sessions.
  • 2
    Ask "Why" more
    4 of 92 transcripts showed any probing (~4%). The target is ≥1 "Why?" or "Tell me more" per key answer — every interviewer.
  • 3
    Stop showing the questions
    Interviewers reading off the form creates leading responses. Confirmed in recordings — brief all interviewers before next session.
