7 Three Sub-Personas

Behavioral clusters emerging from interest, pay, and comfort data

The Hustler ~15–20%

  • Entrepreneurial mindset, already earning from gigs or freelance
  • Wants uncapped earnings, leaderboards, competitive environment
  • Minimum expectation: PKR 40–60K
"40 shops in two weeks is easy if you have the strategy."

The Cautious Pragmatist ~50–55%

  • Interested but risk-averse — needs a safety net
  • Needs base salary + clear expectations before committing
  • Minimum expectation: PKR 30–40K
"Pure commission is scary because I might work for 5 hours and earn zero."

The Reluctant Observer ~10–15%

  • Academically focused, views field sales as beneath their station
  • Would need internship / research framing to consider it
  • Requires fixed salary — will not accept performance-based pay
"Why doing it after graduating from renowned university."

8 Transcript Insights

Key findings from 43 voice recordings NOT captured in form responses
1. Form entries lose nuance. Mahrukh gave a 4,900-char interview about startups and agency work. Her form says "Nothing" for first reaction. The form captures direction but loses specificity.
2. Amna is missing from the dataset. She has a recording but no form entry. Her data was never entered.
3. Students can see the questions. In at least 2 recordings, the interviewer showed the question list to the student, priming their answers.
4. Zero probing across all 43 recordings. Interviewers read the next question rather than following up on interesting answers. Rich tangents (Mahrukh's startups, Mickyle's cold calling, Huzaifa's stock trading) are never explored.
5. Market exhaustion concern. Talha raised a point no one else did: "Getting paid per merchant will keep on decreasing because the number of shops are limited."
6. Renuka frames the role as "digital education, not sales." This reframing could be the messaging breakthrough — positioning GPs as educators rather than salespeople changes the identity calculus entirely.
7. IBA students calculate ROI, not just express interest. AhmedHassaan worked it out unprompted: "100 visits × 30 minutes = 50 hours. So I'll be expecting at least 1,000 rupees per hour, which means 50,000." Susheel independently landed at 35–45K minimum. No Szabist student did this math. IBA treats this as a financial decision, not a gut-feel call.
8. Title sensitivity is sharper at IBA. AhmedHassaan: "Brand ambassador — that's too undermining. I'd go more towards salesman." Haider already captured this in Card 9. The pattern across IBA is consistent: professional framing matters, and "ambassador" sounds junior. "Growth Partner" or "Territory Manager" are the preferred labels.
9. Prior gig experience narrows the pitch window. Haider tried a delivery app: "With the petrol price the saving was almost zero." These students have live comparisons to freelancing, tutoring, and delivery gigs. The offer has to beat Careem margins and Fiverr flexibility — not just sound better than nothing.
10. IBA students self-screen on criteria the form never captures. AhmedHassaan asked about shop types unprompted ("18+ shops, cigarettes, tobacco — I'd avoid those"). Haider asked for an ID card for market credibility. MuhammadSaadiq asked for a certificate and a career path. These filters only appear in recordings, not forms.
